Featured Plant

Diospyros Virginiana:The American Persimmon

Growing Tips for American Persimmons:
  • Choose a self-fertile female tree such as Garretson or Meader (otherwise you'll have to plant both male and female trees).
  • Plant the tree deep into the ground and preferably in the Spring.
  • Place compost around the trunk of the tree.
  • Don't pick too soon! Ripe persimmons are colorful and soft!

Captain John Smith of the Jamestown colony wrote in 1607,the fruit is like a medlar; it is first green, then yellow and red when it is ripe: if it is not ripe, it will drive a man's mouth awrie with much torment, but when it is ripe, it is as delicious as the apricot.

The History of the American Persimmon

The American persimmon is native to the eastern coast of America. When ripe, the persimmon is a sweet little fruit. Not only does it taste delicious, but it also contains nutrients such as Vitamin C, calcium, iron and potassium. According to Dr. Christopher Briand (a plant biology profesor at Salisbury University, MD), this fruit served many purposes in our history's past. Briand explains that persimmons were eaten by both Native Americans and early American settlers. The colonist learned to make breads, pudding, syrup, and pies with persimmons. Another favorite of the English and Swedish settlers was to make persimmon beer.

During the Civil War, soldiers found many uses for this amazing tree. Soliders used persimmon seeds to replace buttons and brew a coffee substitute. Soldiers also found a way to turn persimmons into brandy. In addition, the American persimmon could be used for medicinal purposes and the wood was hardy enough for helping create a variety of tools. Colonists even carved the wood of a persimmon tree for the head of a golf-club.

To find out more one can read Professor Briand's fascinating history of the persimmon. The abstract is titled, The Common Persimmon: The history of an underutilized fruit tree (16th–19th centuries)

Featured Farmer

Beginning a Persimmon Tree Farm

C harles Littlefield, working as an international consultant, spent numerous years travelling the world. While working on economic development projects in Guatemala, Charles became interested in native plants. He would ask, "What plants are native to this soil?" Typically, plants that are native to the soil of a specific region grow in abundance. By planting native fruits, vegetables and grains provide a better source of food for more impoverished areas. He also discovered that native plants have greater health benefits to people residing in the same climate. It was concluded that these plants had both a positive impact on the environment and health benefits to their residents and were a clear choice in developing local agriculture.

After a couple of spent years in Guatemala, Charles returned to work in Washington D.C. He soon bought a home in Rockville, Maryland with his wife and two daughters. Charles found out that the state of Maryland had a program that pays residents to remove invasive plant species from their yard and replace them with plants native to the region. The extra money a year for growing native plants was a good incentive. Thus, when Charles found some free time between family and travelling for his job, he began pulling out invasive species and landscaping their yard with native trees and plants.

American persimmons, he discovered, were native to Maryland but had been replaced by apples sent in from China and other fruits eaten today that are not originally from North America. Charles, who grew up in a then rural town in Northern Michigan, could remember going on hikes and spotting wild raspberries, strawberries. mushrooms, etc. Charles planted a couple of persimmon trees and like the early colonist Charles discovered these trees did very well in their native soil.

Charles became involved in the community of Rockville serving on their city council. He looked into Rockville's city development but was particularly interested in urban gardens and for fun started a beer brewing co-op with his neighbors. As he says, "It makes for friendly neighbors." However, Charles was the most passionate about his persimmon trees and his wife and he experimented with a variety of recipes. Finally, a year ago, Charles entered a contest and was one of the people chosen as a farmer for a pilot program. This year Charles planted his first persimmon tree farm in Germantown, MD. Currently, Charles is happily retired from international politics and works at George Washington University where he has more time for family and farming his persimmons.

Charles was recently interviewed by the Washington Post and a great article about his farm was featured in Rockville's local paper. Read more about it at: New Farmers Planted in County with help from Special Program.

Find and Grow Edible Native Plants

Native Plant Soil When Where Identify Nutrients

Morel Mushrooms

 well-drained.sandy soil.ph:4.4 to 7.1 March to May Zone: 4-9. Forest areas around trees. Best to look around dead or dying elm trees oblong mushroom with brown spongy ridges. Sliced lengthwise it is hollow inside. Do not eat if not hollow-poisonous. Vitamin D, Iron, B Vitamins

PawPaws

 deep fertile soil, slightly acidic. ph:5.5-7.0 August to mid-September Zone: 5-8. Not far from rivers. In nature an understory tree. Pawpaws when ripe fall naturally from the tree. Green to yellowish-green and soft when ripe. High in Vitamin C, Magnesium, Iron, Copper, Potassium and other amino-acids.

Saguaro

 Well drained soil Harvest fruit from mid-June to early July Sonoran Desert: The lower part of Arizona and along the Colorado River. Ripe green buds on top of the saguaro. Buds are closed with some pink on the end. Vitamin,fiber and protein.

Saskatoon Berries

 Sandy,well-drained soil. But can be found on dry rocky soils. ph: 6.0 to 7.5 Ripens early to late July Zone: 0-3. Found in open woods on bluffs or hillsides. Along banks, gullies and near streams. Pink to deep purple in color when ripe Antioxidants. Possesses anti-inflammatory properties. Vitamin A,C, Iron and Potassium. Higher levels of protein than other berries

Spruce Trees

Native to the US: Sitka, Brewers, Black, Red, Blue Spruce Varieties. Douglas Fir also great for edible tips 		Adaptable tree. Typically moist drained soil that is loose or sandy. Some varieties are drought-tolerant. Spring Zone: 2-8. Found in the forest. Black: (common) US Zones 2-6. Brewers: (Rare) Klammath Mt. Pacific Coast Red: Northeast. Blue: Rocky Mts. Sitka: Alaska Pacific Coast Zones 7,8. Tips are soft, bright green when ripe. Can pop tips off like berries (remove brown casing). Vitamin C. Contains Electrolytes (like gatorade). Once used to soothe coughs, sore throat and help congestion.

Recipes

Persimmon Beer (1913) Dishes & Beverages Of The Old South Martha McCulloch-Williams

The poor relation of champagne--with the advantage that nobody is ever the worse for drinking it. To make it, take full-ripe persimmons, the juicier the better, free them of stalks and calyxes, then mash thoroughly, and add enough wheat bran or middlings to make a stiffish dough. Form the dough into thin, flat cakes, which bake crisp in a slow oven.

When cold break them up in a clean barrel, and fill it with filtered rainwater. A bushel of persimmons before mashing will make a barrel of beer. Set the barrel upright, covered with a thin cloth, in a warm, dry place, free of taints. Let stand until the beer works--the persimmon cakes will rise and stand in a foamy mass on top.

After three to four weeks, either move the barrel to a cold place, or rack off the beer into bottles or demijohns, tieing down the corks, and keeping the bottled stuff very cool. The more meaty and flavorous the persimmons, the richer will be the beer.

Beware of putting in fruit that has not felt the touch of frost, so retains a rough tang. A very little of it will spoil a whole brewing of beer.

If the beer is left standing in the barrel a wooden cover should be laid over the cloth, after it is done working. Fermentation can be hastened by putting in with the persimmon cakes a slice of toast dipped in quick yeast. But if the temperature is right, the beer will ferment itself.

The complete 'Dishes & Beverages Of The Old South' may be found on the Michigan State University website: Feeding America: The Historic American Cookbook Project.
